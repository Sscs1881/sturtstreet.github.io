Introducing & Exploring Ideas

See, Think, Wonder

Thinking focus: Observation, interpretation, questioning

A strong entry routine for visual prompts, artefacts, stories, science phenomena and inquiry provocations.

Classroom adaptation: Use pictures, real objects and oral responses in R–2; add written evidence and deeper inference from Years 3–6.

How to run the routine

  1. Students carefully observe an image, object, text or phenomenon.
  2. Students describe what they see, without jumping straight to interpretation.
  3. Students explain what they think might be happening and what evidence led them there.
  4. Students generate wonderings or questions for further inquiry.

Classroom example

Year 2 Science – Mini Beasts

Teacher prompt: Show a close-up image of a caterpillar cocoon.

Possible student response: “I see a hard shell hanging from a branch. I think a butterfly might come out. I wonder how long it stays inside?”

Best fit: Reception–Year 6

Introducing & Exploring Ideas

Think, Puzzle, Explore

Thinking focus: Prior knowledge, questions, inquiry planning

Useful at the beginning of an inquiry to surface what students think they know and what they want to investigate.

Classroom adaptation: For younger learners, replace writing with drawing, partner talk and teacher scribing.

How to run the routine

  1. Ask students what they already think they know about the topic.
  2. Ask what puzzles them or what questions they have.
  3. Ask how they might explore or investigate those questions.
  4. Use the responses to shape inquiry groups, resources or next learning steps.

Classroom example

Year 4 HASS – First Contacts

Teacher prompt: Before beginning the unit, ask what students think they know about early contact between Aboriginal peoples and Europeans.

Possible student response: “I think people traded things. I’m puzzled about how they communicated.”

Best fit: Year 2–Year 6

Introducing & Exploring Ideas

Chalk Talk

Thinking focus: Silent discussion, connection-making, questioning

Encourages every student to contribute ideas without needing to speak publicly.

Classroom adaptation: Use picture cards, stickers or teacher-scribed comments for junior primary students.

How to run the routine

  1. Write a question, statement or image in the middle of a large sheet.
  2. Students respond silently by writing comments, questions or connections.
  3. Students read others’ responses and add replies or links.
  4. Finish with a whole-class discussion about patterns, surprises and questions.

Classroom example

Year 5 Health – Belonging

Teacher prompt: Place the question ‘What helps people feel they belong?’ in the centre of the page.

Possible student response: “Someone wrote ‘being included in games’. I added ‘also being listened to when decisions are made’.”

Best fit: Year 3–Year 6

Introducing & Exploring Ideas

Zoom In

Thinking focus: Close observation, prediction, revising thinking

Excellent for slowing down observation and helping students revise their thinking as more information is revealed.

Classroom adaptation: Use images, maps, artworks, historical photos, mystery objects or science diagrams.

How to run the routine

  1. Reveal only a small part of an image, text or object.
  2. Students describe what they notice and predict what the whole might be.
  3. Reveal more sections gradually.
  4. Students revise their thinking and explain what changed.

Classroom example

Reception Inquiry – Community Helpers

Teacher prompt: Reveal part of a firefighter’s helmet, then gradually reveal the whole image.

Possible student response: “At first I thought it was a bike helmet. Now I think it is a firefighter because I can see the yellow coat.”

Best fit: Reception–Year 6

Introducing & Exploring Ideas

3-2-1 Bridge

Thinking focus: Activating prior knowledge, reflection, conceptual change

Helps students compare initial thinking with later thinking after new learning.

Classroom adaptation: In Years 1–2, reduce the structure to 2 words, 1 question and 1 drawing before and after learning.

How to run the routine

  1. Before learning, students record 3 words, 2 questions and 1 metaphor or image.
  2. Teach, read, investigate or explore the topic.
  3. After learning, students complete a second 3-2-1.
  4. Students bridge the two responses by explaining how their thinking changed.

Classroom example

Year 6 Science – Renewable Energy

Teacher prompt: Before and after investigating solar and wind energy, complete a 3-2-1 Bridge.

Possible student response: “I used to think renewable energy meant it never runs out. Now I think it depends on systems, storage and weather.”

Best fit: Year 3–Year 6

Considering Perspectives & Decisions

Compass Points

Thinking focus: Decision-making, concerns, needs, next steps

Useful when students are considering options, issues, changes, design choices or class decisions.

Classroom adaptation: Use the language: Excited, Worried, Need to Know, Next Step.

How to run the routine

  1. Present an idea, proposal, issue or decision.
  2. Students record what excites them about it.
  3. Students record worries or concerns.
  4. Students identify what they need to know and suggest next steps.

Classroom example

Year 3/4 Design – Improving the School Yard

Teacher prompt: Consider the idea of creating a quiet play zone at recess.

Possible student response: “I’m excited because some people need calm spaces. I’m worried it might be too small.”

Best fit: Year 3–Year 6

Synthesising & Organising Ideas

Connect, Extend, Challenge

Thinking focus: Connecting, extending, questioning

Strong for reflecting after reading, viewing, discussion, excursions or new content learning.

Classroom adaptation: For R–2 use: What did this remind you of? What new thing did you learn? What question do you still have?

How to run the routine

  1. Students identify something that connects to what they already know.
  2. Students explain how the new learning extended their thinking.
  3. Students name something that is still challenging, confusing or unresolved.
  4. Use the challenges to plan further teaching or inquiry.

Classroom example

Year 5 Reading – Novel Study

Teacher prompt: After reading a chapter about a character facing exclusion, complete Connect, Extend, Challenge.

Possible student response: “This connects to times people are left out at lunch. It extends my thinking because the character hides how upset he is.”

Best fit: Year 2–Year 6

Synthesising & Organising Ideas

I Used to Think… Now I Think…

Thinking focus: Reflection, metacognition, change over time

Simple, powerful routine for making learning visible at the end of a lesson or inquiry sequence.

Classroom adaptation: Use sentence stems orally for younger learners; ask older students to explain what changed their thinking.

How to run the routine

  1. Ask students to recall what they thought before the learning.
  2. Students complete the stem: I used to think…
  3. Students complete the stem: Now I think…
  4. Students explain what caused their thinking to change.

Classroom example

Year 1 Science – Materials

Teacher prompt: After testing waterproof materials, ask students to reflect on their changed thinking.

Possible student response: “I used to think all fabric lets water through. Now I think some fabric can be waterproof.”

Best fit: Year 1–Year 6

Synthesising & Organising Ideas

Headlines

Thinking focus: Summarising, identifying the main idea

Helps students distil the heart of an idea into a short statement.

Classroom adaptation: In junior primary, students can choose from picture headlines or co-create a class headline.

How to run the routine

  1. After learning, ask students to write a short headline that captures the main idea.
  2. Students share headlines with a partner or group.
  3. Students explain why their headline fits the learning.
  4. Revise headlines after discussion if needed.

Classroom example

Year 4 HASS – Water Use

Teacher prompt: Write a headline that captures today’s learning about water conservation.

Possible student response: “Every Drop Counts: Small Choices Can Save Big Water.”

Best fit: Year 2–Year 6

Synthesising & Organising Ideas

CSI: Colour, Symbol, Image

Thinking focus: Symbolic thinking, synthesis, explanation

Encourages students to represent big ideas visually and justify their choices.

Classroom adaptation: Younger students can choose one colour or image only and explain orally.

How to run the routine

  1. Students choose a colour that represents the idea or topic.
  2. Students choose a symbol that captures an important meaning.
  3. Students choose or draw an image that represents the big idea.
  4. Students explain the reasoning behind each choice.

Classroom example

Year 6 English – Theme in Literature

Teacher prompt: Represent the theme of courage using a colour, symbol and image.

Possible student response: “I chose orange because it feels brave, a bridge because courage helps you cross fear, and an image of a student speaking up.”

Best fit: Year 3–Year 6

Digging Deeper into Ideas

Claim, Support, Question

Thinking focus: Reasoning, evidence, uncertainty

Builds evidence-based reasoning in HASS, science, reading and inquiry investigations.

Classroom adaptation: Use simpler stems: I think…, My clue is…, I still wonder…

How to run the routine

  1. Students make a claim about a text, source, issue or investigation.
  2. Students support the claim with evidence.
  3. Students identify a question or uncertainty that remains.
  4. Students review whether the evidence is strong enough.

Classroom example

Year 5 Science – States of Matter

Teacher prompt: Make a claim about what happened when the ice cube was heated.

Possible student response: “Claim: Heat changed the solid into a liquid. Support: The ice melted into water.”

Best fit: Year 4–Year 6

Digging Deeper into Ideas

What Makes You Say That?

Thinking focus: Evidence, explanation, accountable talk

A foundational teacher prompt that strengthens reasoning and helps students justify interpretations.

Classroom adaptation: Use constantly in discussion: What did you notice that makes you think that?

How to run the routine

  1. Ask students to share an idea, answer or interpretation.
  2. Prompt them with: What makes you say that?
  3. Students identify evidence, clues or reasoning.
  4. Invite others to agree, add evidence or offer a different interpretation.

Classroom example

Reception Literacy – Picture Book Discussion

Teacher prompt: A student says, ‘The character is sad.’ Ask: What makes you say that?

Possible student response: “Because his mouth is down and he is sitting by himself.”

Best fit: Reception–Year 6

Perspective-taking

Circle of Viewpoints

Thinking focus: Perspective, empathy, voice, complexity

Useful for HASS, literature, ethical questions, community issues and historical inquiry.

Classroom adaptation: Use role cards and oral sentence stems for younger learners.

How to run the routine

  1. Identify an issue, event, text or situation with multiple viewpoints.
  2. List different people, groups or objects connected to the issue.
  3. Students speak or write from one viewpoint using sentence stems.
  4. Discuss how different perspectives change understanding of the issue.

Classroom example

Year 4 HASS – Local Development

Teacher prompt: Consider a proposal to build a new playground from different viewpoints.

Possible student response: “I am thinking as a neighbour. I like children having somewhere to play, but I worry about noise.”

Best fit: Year 3–Year 6

Perspective-taking

Step Inside

Thinking focus: Empathy, imagination, perspective

Supports students to imagine the thoughts, feelings and motivations of a character, person or object.

Classroom adaptation: Excellent with picture books, history images, artefacts and drama.

How to run the routine

  1. Choose a character, person, animal, object or historical figure.
  2. Students imagine what that viewpoint might see or notice.
  3. Students imagine what the viewpoint might know, believe or care about.
  4. Students imagine what the viewpoint might wonder or need.

Classroom example

Year 3 English – Character Study

Teacher prompt: Step inside the main character after they move to a new school.

Possible student response: “I can see everyone already has friends. I feel nervous. I wonder who will sit with me at lunch.”

Best fit: Year 1–Year 6

Exploring Art, Images & Objects

Looking Ten Times Two

Thinking focus: Careful observation, description, detail

Trains students to slow down, notice details and return to a stimulus more than once.

Classroom adaptation: For R–2, ask for 3–5 observations first; build up to 10 over time.

How to run the routine

  1. Students look quietly at an image, artwork, object or scene.
  2. Students list several things they notice.
  3. Students look again more carefully.
  4. Students add more observations, aiming for detail rather than speed.

Classroom example

Year 1 Visual Arts – Responding to Artwork

Teacher prompt: Look at the painting once, list what you notice, then look again and add more details.

Possible student response: “First I saw a tree and a house. Then I noticed tiny people, smoke and shadows.”

Best fit: Reception–Year 6

Synthesising & Organising Ideas

Generate, Sort, Connect, Elaborate

Thinking focus: Concept mapping, relationships, elaboration

Strong for organising complex ideas and showing relationships between concepts.

Classroom adaptation: Use pre-made word/image cards for Years 2–3 before expecting independent concept maps.

How to run the routine

  1. Students generate words or ideas connected to a topic.
  2. Students sort the ideas into groups or categories.
  3. Students connect related ideas with lines or arrows.
  4. Students elaborate by adding explanations, examples or evidence.

Classroom example

Year 6 HASS – Democracy

Teacher prompt: Generate ideas connected to democracy, then sort and connect them.

Possible student response: “I connected voting to representation because voting is how people choose who speaks for them.”

Best fit: Year 4–Year 6

Investigating Objects & Systems

Parts, Purposes, Complexities

Thinking focus: Systems thinking, function, complexity

Useful for science, technology, design, geography, school systems and community systems.

Classroom adaptation: For younger children, use real objects and ask: What parts can you see? What does each part do?

How to run the routine

  1. Choose an object, system, process or place to investigate.
  2. Students identify its parts.
  3. Students explain the purpose of each part.
  4. Students identify complexities, interactions or problems within the system.

Classroom example

Year 3 Technology – Bicycles

Teacher prompt: Investigate the parts, purposes and complexities of a bicycle.

Possible student response: “The chain helps turn the wheel. The brakes slow the bike.”

Best fit: Year 3–Year 6

Generating Possibilities

Creative Questions

Thinking focus: Questioning, possibility, curiosity

Develops richer questions for inquiry, design thinking and student-led investigations.

Classroom adaptation: Provide question stems such as: What if…? How might…? Why would…?

How to run the routine

  1. Start with a topic, problem, text or phenomenon.
  2. Students generate many possible questions using open-ended stems.
  3. Students improve closed questions into richer inquiry questions.
  4. Students select the strongest questions for investigation.

Classroom example

Year 2 Inquiry – School Gardens

Teacher prompt: Generate creative questions about how we could improve the school garden.

Possible student response: “How might we make a garden that helps butterflies, gives shade and is easy for children to look after?”

Best fit: Year 2–Year 6
